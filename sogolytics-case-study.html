The Bottleneck

Insight Poverty at Scale

Legacy reports were data-heavy and required manual configuration. Users spent hours "finding" the story instead of "fixing" the problem. Feedback programs were underutilized and insights perpetually delayed.

The Trust Gap

Black-Box AI is a Liability

In regulated environments — K–12 districts, Credit Unions, Healthcare networks — users refused to trust a summary if they couldn't see the evidence. Compliance officers treated AI as a risk, not a tool.

The Market Stakes

Competing with Incumbents

With AI Analytics in the XM market valued at $3–6B, the platform needed a prescriptive solution to compete with enterprise incumbents. Generic dashboards were no longer a sufficient differentiator.

AX · Trust design

The Evidence Layer

To solve the trust gap in regulated industries, I designed a "Show Source" trigger for every AI-generated statement. Users drill from executive summary to supporting anonymised data in a single click — without leaving the report view. For a Healthcare Admin, this means reading a "Staff Responsiveness" finding and instantly seeing the 12 patient comments behind it.

Design decision Inline expansion, not a modal — keeps the user anchored to the narrative while they verify. The interaction reinforces that each AI claim must earn trust independently, not inherit it from the report as a whole.
AX rationale — I designed this as a per-claim trust contract, not a UI pattern. "Show Source" defines the condition under which the AI is permitted to be acted on. This is the difference between a useful AI feature and a trusted one in a HIPAA-regulated environment.
🎯
AX · Behavior design

Role-Based Narratives

The same underlying survey data produces different outputs depending on who is reading it and in which vertical. A Credit Union CX Manager sees "Member Retention Risk" framing. A School Superintendent sees "Stakeholder Sentiment" language. I designed the conditional logic that governs these adaptations before any interface work began.

Design decision Vertical context and role are declared at session level, not inferred — ensuring the AI never defaults to generic language in a high-stakes regulated environment.
AX rationale — These are named behavior rules, not personalisation features. Specifying what the AI does differently per context — before engineering begins — is what makes the system predictable at scale across Healthcare, Credit Unions, and K–12.
📐
AX · Oversight design

Progressive Disclosure

I designed a three-layer information architecture that lets users choose how much of the AI's reasoning they need to see. Layer 1 surfaces the narrative headline. Layer 2 opens the evidence. Layer 3 exposes raw data and filters. The structure reduces cognitive load for executives while preserving full depth for analysts — without building two separate products.

Design decision Top: AI summary & headlines · Middle: Plain-language visual insights · Deep: Raw charts and filters · Each layer transitions seamlessly — no modal interruptions, no dead ends.
AX rationale — Each layer is also an oversight mode: L1 is lights-out (agent acts, human consumes), L2 is supervised (human verifies each claim), L3 is full override (human leads the analysis). I designed this as a human-control spectrum, not just an information hierarchy.
🛡️
AX · Constraint design

Compliance by Design

For healthcare, K–12, and financial services clients, compliance is non-negotiable. I designed configurable PII redaction, audience-specific masking, and export audit trails as defaults — not settings. Every AI-generated report is compliant before it reaches a user, not after they configure it to be.

Design decision HIPAA (Healthcare) · FERPA (K–12) · Financial Services compliance · Audit-ready export logs — compliance confirmation visible at the point of export, not buried in settings.
AX rationale — HIPAA and FERPA are hard constraints on what the AI is permitted to output — not UI guardrails. Designing them as operating boundaries rather than configuration options is what enabled legal and compliance sign-off on the system before launch.

What I Learned

01

AI is only valuable if users trust it. Designing transparency as a first-class deliverable — not an afterthought — is what separates an AI feature from a trusted one in regulated industries.

AX signal — Per-claim trust gates, not report-level trust grants.
02

Progressive disclosure is more than an information pattern. When designing AI systems, each layer is a deliberate choice about how much control to give the human — and that choice should be made consciously, not by default.

AX signal — L1 = lights-out · L2 = supervised · L3 = full override.
03

Insights must be tailored to roles, not just data sets. The same data means something different to a CX Manager and a Compliance Officer — and the AI's framing should reflect that before any interface is designed.

AX signal — Role and vertical are behavior policy branches, not UI variants.
04

Compliance is a design input, not a handoff. In regulated verticals, the conditions under which an AI is permitted to output data are UX decisions as much as engineering ones.

AX signal — HIPAA and FERPA define the agent's operating boundary — not its interface defaults.
05

The interface is the trust bridge between model and human. In AI-driven products, the UX designer's job expands to include designing how the system's reasoning becomes legible enough to be trusted — or overridden.

AX signal — Human override must be a visible, designed path — not an afterthought.
06

Vertical specialisation is the strongest moat. Depth beats breadth in mid-market XM — and designing for the specific vocabulary and risk model of each vertical is what competitors building generic tools cannot easily replicate.

AX signal — Dual legibility — human-readable narrative and machine-readable audit log — makes vertical depth defensible.
