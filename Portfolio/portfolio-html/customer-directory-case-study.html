Project Overview

A CRM-integrated data management system for regulated industries

The Customer Directory was built for credit unions, healthcare networks, and K–12 institutions — letting these organisations manage contact lists, build segments, and trigger targeted surveys. I owned all design decisions end-to-end: information architecture, interaction design, usability research, stakeholder alignment, and a 47-component design system.

The project's defining challenge wasn't UI complexity. It was identifying who we were actually designing for — before we shipped the wrong answer at scale.

⚠ User Pain Points
  • 3.2 hrs lost per survey launch to data prep outside the platform
  • Non-technical users forced to think like database admins
  • 73% of support tickets from import failures
↑ Business Goals
  • Grow ARR in credit union, healthcare, and K–12 verticals
  • Reduce churn through feature stickiness
  • Accelerate plan upgrade rate
⊘ Constraints
  • Q3 launch deadline
  • 6 weeks + $180K already invested in v1
  • 90-day activity history cap (infrastructure)
  • Must serve IT admins without blocking non-technical users
◎ Success Metrics
  • >70% primary user feature adoption
  • Reduce import-related support tickets
  • Reduce survey launch time by >40%
  • >60 SUS on core flows
Competitive Analysis

The market solves the wrong problem for the wrong user

Before committing to a direction, I audited five direct and adjacent competitors across the same three verticals — credit unions, healthcare, and K–12. The goal wasn't to find features to copy, but to identify where the category was failing primary users and where an opinionated design decision could create durable differentiation.

Research Method

Hands-on product walkthroughs for each competitor's import and contact management flow. Evaluated against three primary user tasks: (1) upload a CSV, (2) build a filtered segment, (3) trigger a survey on a contact action. Scored independently, then cross-referenced with interview data from 32 user sessions.

Lens of Analysis

Not feature completeness — that's a trap. The lens was: can a non-technical marketing coordinator at a credit union complete these three tasks without IT help, within 30 minutes, on day one? That single bar surfaced more meaningful differentiation than any feature matrix.

Three design opportunities the market left open

The competitive audit didn't just validate the problem — it gave each design decision a competitive rationale.

OPPORTUNITY 01
No one auto-detects industry-specific fields
Every competitor puts field mapping on the user. Mailchimp is the most accessible — but even it doesn't recognise "Member ID" or "Patient Name" as special. Intelligent detection of vertical-specific patterns was an uncontested design space.
→ Became: Intelligent Detection principle
OPPORTUNITY 02
Dynamic segments exist — but require CRM training
HubSpot and Qualtrics have smart lists. But their filter builders assume CRM fluency. No competitor offered auto-updating segments with a conversational, plain-language UI accessible to a non-technical daily operator.
→ Became: Smart Segments feature
OPPORTUNITY 03
Nobody lets you try before IT unlocks the gate
Every platform requires real data before a user can experience value. For regulated industries with IT gatekeeping, this is a critical abandonment point. No competitor offered a realistic sample data preview for the user's specific vertical context.
→ Became: Preview Mode (highest-leverage feature)
The competitive analysis didn't change the problem statement — it sharpened it. Every gap traced back to the same root: the market was designing for IT buyers, not daily operators. That finding reinforced the research direction and gave the pivot a competitive frame, not just a usability one.

Four insights that forced a rethink

INSIGHT 01
Secondary users gave polished beta feedback. We weighted it most.
"I don't know what a foreign key is." — Marketing Coordinator
→ Design for daily operators first. Build a separate non-default path for technical config.
INSIGHT 02
0% task completion on Schema Builder. Users abandoned at 12 minutes.
Primary users think in data they have — not structures they need to design.
→ Detect structure from uploaded data. Don't require users to architect it upfront.
INSIGHT 03
68% abandoned onboarding before importing data.
Perceived risk of "breaking something" exceeded actual complexity.
→ Sample data preview in the user's own industry removes the leap-of-faith barrier.
INSIGHT 04
73% of segmentation decisions were based on recent customer actions.
Users can't rebuild lists manually every week.
→ Auto-updating "alive" segments — not static snapshots.

Feature Deep Dives

Three features — and the decisions behind them

01 — Smart Segments: lists that stay alive

Diary studies showed 73% of segmentation decisions were based on recent customer actions. Users rebuilt the same lists manually every week.

Real examples from user interviews

  • "Members who opened accounts in last 30 days but haven't set up mobile banking"
  • "Patients discharged from cardiology in last 7 days with no follow-up scheduled"
  • "Parents who haven't responded to the last 2 surveys"

Key design decisions

  • Conversational activity badges: "+12 new members today" — not raw data counts
  • Simple timeline trend — not a complex analytics graph
  • Alerts only for dramatic shifts (>20% change) to avoid notification fatigue
  • A/B tested with 240 beta users — segment reuse increased 340%

02 — Activity Triggers: IF this → THEN that

73% of survey triggers in diary studies were based on recent customer actions. I designed a recipe-card UI matching mental models from Mailchimp and Constant Contact — tools primary users already knew.

  • Drag-and-drop blocks with full keyboard alternatives (WCAG 2.1 AA)
  • Live preview: "X customers would have received this in the last 30 days"
  • Plain-language frequency warnings: "These members will receive 3 surveys in 14 days"
  • Industry-specific templates out of the box — no blank canvas

03 — Activity History: turning a constraint into a feature

⚠ Infrastructure Constraint

Database costs limited activity retention to 90 days. Silently deleting data wasn't an option for regulated industries.

→ Design Response

Key Moments: users flag up to 50 activities per contact for permanent retention. Beta users flagged an average of 12. The 90-day constraint became invisible.

Scope Decisions

Three things I chose not to build — and why

Knowing what to cut is as important as knowing what to ship.

✕ Advanced Data Transformation Tools
Only 12% of primary users had the skills for this — and they preferred Excel for complex transforms anyway. 4–6 week delay to serve a minority with a working solution.
Evidence: skill assessment · 32 interviews
✕ Real-Time Collaboration
94% of users worked individually. Shipped version history and "last edited by" indicators instead — the safety net without co-editing overhead.
Evidence: diary study · 94% solo usage
✕ Industry-Specific Data Models at Launch
Would have delayed launch 4–6 months. Smart detection recognised 87% of common field patterns. Healthcare templates shipped in Phase 2 after adoption data confirmed fit.
Shipped: Phase 2 · post-adoption data
Scope cuts recovered 3 weeks of the pivot delay — bringing a 6-week delay down to 3.
Reflection

What this project changed about how I work

1
Design for primary users. Accommodate secondary users.
Secondary users are more articulate. But primary users are the ones who come back on Tuesday, and Thursday, and Monday after that. Optimise for daily operators and build escape hatches for edge cases.
2
Test early with the right users — not just any users.
We invested 6 weeks before testing with primary users. Paper prototypes with 6 primary users in Week 2 would have surfaced this cleanly. I now require 3 primary user tests with low-fidelity prototypes in the first two weeks of every project.
3
Mental models beat feature completeness.
The Schema Builder was more powerful. It failed because it didn't match how primary users thought. Before adding features I now ask: does this match how primary users already think? If not — change the feature, not the documentation.
4
Sample data removes the leap of faith.
For non-technical users in regulated industries, perceived risk often exceeds actual complexity. Preview Mode with realistic sample data was the single highest-impact design decision in the project.
5
Frame design decisions in the language of the room.
I didn't convince leadership with "better UX." I framed it as a $2.2M annual revenue decision. The same design argument can be made in three languages — lead with the one that lands.
