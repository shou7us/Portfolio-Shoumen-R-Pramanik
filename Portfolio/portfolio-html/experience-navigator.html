01 — Project Overview

From abstract goal to operational programme

Experience Navigator is an AI-powered strategy generation tool built for mid-to-senior CX and EX leaders who are accountable for business outcomes but lack the time and specialist depth to build cross-functional programmes from scratch.

The core problem was what I framed as the Strategic Execution Gap — organisations routinely set high-level goals like "reduce churn" or "improve onboarding" but have no systematic way to convert those intentions into operational plans, survey logic, and measurable KPIs. I led UX across a 3-person team (1 designer, 1 PM, 1 developer) over a 3-month sprint, designing the end-to-end experience from goal input to a fully generated, section-by-section strategy output.

Design Context

Enterprise clients in healthcare, financial services, and K–12 education share a critical constraint: they carry high compliance exposure, operate with lean internal CX/EX teams, and face board-level scrutiny on any technology investment. A credit union CX director presenting a new feedback programme has no margin for a strategy that looks generic or unproven. Experience Navigator was designed with this enterprise credibility bar in mind — every output section, from vertical-specific pillars to ROI signals, was shaped to hold up in a room with a board, a Chief Experience Officer, or a compliance team.

03 — User Journey Map

Designing for the manager under pressure

The product serves a B2B dual persona — the buyer (VP/C-suite) and the daily user (CX/EX manager). I designed for the manager's journey, with output format shaped to help them get buy-in upward.

Stage Awareness Goal Setting Strategy Gen Review & Act Programme Launch
Manager Actions Hears about tool via PM Selects industry, vertical, goal Watches strategy build section by section Reviews 10 sections, edits as needed Shares output with stakeholders
Manager Pain ↓ Sceptical — "another AI gimmick" Worried choice won't produce relevant output Anxious — "Will this be actually useful?" ↓↓↓ HIGH — Does output hold up in meeting?← Highest friction point ↓
Opportunity — — Live streaming builds trust in real-time Exec Summary + ROI as closer ← HERE —
Key tension I designed against: The highest-friction moment is not generation — it's the review. A VP seeing a wall of AI text loses trust immediately. The section-by-section streaming + collapsible survey cards directly addressed this by making the output feel curated, not dumped.
04 — Key Insights

What the research revealed

01
Insight 1 — The Translation Problem
ObservationTeams don't fail at strategy — they fail at translation. The gap between a PDF strategy and a running survey is where programmes die.
User needTo receive not just a plan, but the operational artefacts (survey logic, KPIs, distribution timing) in a single output.
Product implicationThe design must present strategy and execution together — not sequentially, not in separate tools.
02
Insight 2 — Vertical Blindness
ObservationGeneric CX/EX strategies erode user trust instantly. HR leaders recognise "boilerplate" on sight.
User needTo see their specific industry, vertical, and company scope reflected in every output section — not just the title.
Product implicationWizard inputs must act as deep context parameters, not just filters. Industry + vertical + scope must visibly shape the language, KPIs, and competitive benchmarks in the output.
03
Insight 3 — The Buy-in Blocker
ObservationStakeholder buy-in is blocked not by bad strategy, but by unclear ROI timelines.
User needTo walk into a board conversation with a quantified, time-anchored investment case — not a slide saying "CX matters."
Product implicationROI & Investment must be a first-class output section. Positioned last — after credibility is built — to function as the closer.
04
Insight 4 — The Closed Loop Gap
ObservationSurvey logic is where most CX tools stop — they give you the survey but not what to do when results come in.
User needA closed feedback loop: metric → threshold → action if met → follow-up survey if missed.
Product implicationSurvey recommendations must be branching, not flat. Each survey card must contain the "if/then" logic that tells a manager what to do next — without a data scientist.
05 — Decision Framework

How I chose sequential streaming

The most consequential design decision was how to structure the output.

OptionProsConsDecision Rationale
A: Single large output
Generate all at once		 Feels "complete" on first view Overwhelming — kills trust before user reads —
B: Tab-based sections
User clicks each tab		 Clean navigation · familiar pattern No sense of completeness · user doesn't know what they're getting —
C: Sequential streaming
10 agents, section by sectionCHOSEN		 Builds anticipation · trust builds as each section completes · feels like expert work in real time User can't jump ahead · requires patience Streaming transforms AI output from "black box result" to "watch it think." Each section completing is a micro-trust signal. For sceptical VPs, this matters.

Survey Flow Card — Branching Logic Decision

I chose a vertical pipeline layout over a flat list because CX managers need to see the sequence, not just the components. The colour-coded met/missed states made the "if/then" logic scannable without reading every word.

10 — Opportunity

Three layers of value, designed sequentially

No tool currently occupies the space between "I have a business goal" and "I have a running feedback programme." That space is currently filled by consultants, PowerPoint decks, and months of internal alignment work.

0–6 Months
Layer 1 — The Immediate Unlock
Serve the manager who has a board meeting in two weeks. The 2-minute generation time is the primary value proposition — not AI sophistication, but time compression.
Design constraint I built againstWizard must stay at 6 steps. Complexity at input = abandonment before generation.
6–18 Months
Layer 2 — The Retention Hook
Distribution timing, metric tracking, and follow-up survey logic are embedded in each Survey Flow Card — natural expansion path into programme management.
Design constraint I built againstSurvey cards must contain enough operational detail to run the programme directly from the output.
18+ Months
Layer 3 — The Platform Play
Consultant-facing tier — faster first-draft generation per engagement, shareable outputs, multi-client workspace. Higher WTP, lower churn than mid-market manager.
Design constraint I built againstOutput format must be clean enough to share externally — no raw JSON visible, no AI disclaimers in generated content.

12 — Reflection

Honest retrospective

✓ What Worked
Using vibe coding (rapid AI-assisted prototyping) to validate the core interaction model before committing to a Figma spec. Building a working prototype first meant the PM and developer debated specifics — not abstract concept questions. That velocity created alignment faster than a presentation deck would have.
→ What I'd Improve
The wizard currently treats all 6 inputs equally. In practice, Vertical and Objective are the two variables that most shape output quality. I'd redesign the wizard to give these two more weight — perhaps a two-stage flow where they are selected first, with the others as refinement options.
◆ What This Opens Next
The current product generates strategy. The natural next problem is strategy adoption — how do you help a VP track whether the 12-month roadmap is being executed across a team? That's a dashboarding and accountability layer that sits above this tool, and it's the product problem I'd want to tackle next.
